Synopsis

This report analyzes data from the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administrations (NOAA) storm database. The goal of this report is twofold: 1. To determine which weather event, in the given database, is most harmful to human health. 2. Of the recorded weather events, which events are correlated to have the greatest amount of economic consequences. Economic consequences is used vaguely here, as one could analyze the oppurtunity cost of lost lives or crops, or one could merely ignore consequences of a lost. Given the dataset this author chose to focus on economic damage, in place of economic consequences. It was found that the tornado is the most costly weather event in terms of human health. This was a consequence of the simple model that was chosen - that of a sum between deaths and injuries. As such, tornadoes produce a large amount of injuries; by dominating the injury variable in the model the tornado becomes the weather event that produces the largest human Impact. The most costly weather event was found the be a flood. The flood weather event dominated the model in similar fashion to the tornado, only this time it the property damage varaible was maximized, instead of injuries. In sum, both of these results imply that the dataset should be analyzed in terms of geographic data, because floods and tornadoes are location specific.

This analysis is focused on the EVTYPE (Event Type) variable, as such it must be cleaned in order to be useful. First of all we cannot have THUNDERSTORM and TSTM mean the same thing in our analysis. Also, white spaces and special characters must be removed, and any other EVTYPE that does not make sense for this analysis:

#remove special characters
library(dplyr)
df$EVTYPE <- gsub("[[:punct:]]", "", df$EVTYPE)
df$EVTYPE <- tolower(df$EVTYPE)

#remove summaries, monthly/weekly summaries
patternsToRemove <- c("monthly", "?")
greplString <- paste(patternsToRemove, collapse = "|")
df <- df[(grepl(greplString, df$EVTYPE)),]

#remove everything that has summary in it
df <- df[(!grepl("summary", df$EVTYPE)),]

#turn TSTM into Thunderstorm
tstorm <- c("thunderstorms", "thunderstormw", "tstm", "thunderstorminds", "thunderstorminds", "thunderstrom", "thundertorm", "thundertsorm", "thundestorm", "thunerstorm", "thunderstorminds")
tstormGsub <- paste(tstorm, collapse = "|")
df$EVTYPE <- gsub(tstormGsub, "thunderstorm", df$EVTYPE)
df$EVTYPE <- factor(df$EVTYPE)

Now clean the economic data by converting the PROPDMGEXP and CROPDMGEXP variables to scientific notation, concatenating with PROPDMG/CROPDMG, and generating the final DMG variables

fixDMG <- function(x){
#function takes a list, loops through the list converting values to scientific
#notation, converts list class to character to anticipate future paste call
    x <- toupper(x)
    x <- gsub("[[:punct:]]", "", x)
    x <- lapply(toupper(x), function(x) 
        ifelse(x == "K", "000", 
               ifelse(x =="M", "000000", 
                      ifelse(x == "B", "000000000", x))))
    
    x <- as.character(unlist(x))
    x
}
df$CROPDMGEXP <- fixDMG(df$CROPDMGEXP)
df$PROPDMGEXP <- fixDMG(df$PROPDMGEXP)

#convert and concatenate damage variables
df$PROPDMG <- as.character(df$PROPDMG)
df$CROPDMG <- as.character(df$CROPDMG)
df$propertyDamage <- as.numeric(paste(df$PROPDMG, df$PROPDMGEXP, sep = ""))
df$cropDamage <- as.numeric(paste(df$CROPDMG, df$CROPDMGEXP, sep = ""))

Model Definition

To asses the impact of Climate Events to human health and the Economy I will be using a simple sum model. Thus INJURIES and FATALITIES will be summed across the all time periods in the data frame. The same type of model will be used to analyze the economic damage as well. I prefer to use the dplyr package for operations like this:

#aggregate for human impact analysis
library(dplyr)

populationHealth <- df %>%
                       group_by(EVTYPE) %>%
                            summarise(humanInjuries = 
                                        sum(INJURIES, na.rm = TRUE),
                                      humanDeaths = 
                                        sum(FATALITIES, na.rm = TRUE), 
                                      total =
                                        sum(INJURIES,
                                            FATALITIES, na.rm = TRUE)) %>%
                                arrange(desc(total))

#aggregate for economic impact
econHealth <- df %>%
                group_by(EVTYPE) %>%
                    summarise(propertyImpact = 
                                  sum(propertyDamage, na.rm = TRUE),
                              cropImpact =
                                  sum(cropDamage, na.rm = TRUE),
                              total = 
                                sum(cropDamage,
                                    propertyDamage, na.rm = TRUE)) %>%
                        arrange(desc(total))

Taking the plots in order: The most costly weather event in terms of human wellbeing (defined as the sum of injuries and deaths) is the occurance of a tornado. Tornados dominate the dataset by an order of magnitude in injury count. As this model treats an injury equal to a death this assymetry causes the model to conclude the tornado as the most costly weather event to human wellbeing. In the future this model could be revised, or merely reduced to a peerly economic analysis; while some might find equating a life to a price value the oppurtunity cost of a death is substantial and of economic importance, so that analysis may have some merit.

Turning to the economic analysis, the most costly weather event is a flood. For similary reasons to the tornado, the flood dominates the model by maximizing the property impact variable. The results of flood and tornados dominating the model leads me to think that the dataset may be biased towards the southwest. I would certainly expect it to be biased towards population centers - as there is no health nor economic impact without people. Future analyses may try to analyze this dataset in relation to geographic or socioeconomic data; or for that matter reduce to the cropImpact veriable to a property Impact metric, as a crop is a form of property.
